# Verb Stem Alternation - Literature Review

This report surveys previous scholarship on verb stem alternation (Stem I/II, Form I/II) in Tedim Chin and related Kuki-Chin languages, organized chronologically.

---

## Overview

Verb stem alternation is one of the most discussed morphological phenomena in Kuki-Chin languages. All major descriptions of Tedim Chin identify two distinct verb forms whose distribution is governed by grammatical context. The terminology varies:

| Scholar | Year | "Basic" Form | "Derived" Form |
|---------|------|--------------|----------------|
| Henderson | 1965 | Form I | Form II |
| Zam Ngaih Cing | 2018 | Stem 1 | Stem 2 |
| Button (PNC) | 2011 | FORM-I | FORM-II |
| Singh (Sukte) | 2018 | (implicit) | (implicit) |

## Henderson 1965

**Source:** Henderson, Eugénie J.A. 1965. *Tiddim Chin: A Descriptive Analysis of Two Texts*. London: Oxford University Press. pp. 72–89.

Henderson provides the foundational description of verb stem alternation in Tedim Chin, which she terms "Form I" and "Form II." Her analysis is primarily phonological but with clear grammatical conditioning.

### Definition

> "Verbs are distinguished from all other classes of words by their formal scatter. All verbs have two alternating forms, dependent upon grammatical context. In the great majority of the verbs recorded one of these forms, hereafter referred to as Form II, can always be predicted from the other, hereafter referred to as Form I. Such verbs may be termed regular. All other verbs are irregular." (p. 72)

### Grammatical Distribution

Henderson identifies clear distributional patterns:

> "In the narrative style, the final predicative phrase of all conclusive sentences contains a verb or verbs in Form I. Such sentences are said to be characterized by the indicative mood. In the same style, the final verbal phrase of all inconclusive sentences contains a verb or verbs in Form II. Such sentences are said to be characterized by the subjunctive mood." (p. 29)

Key environments:
- **Form I**: Final predicative phrase of conclusive sentences (indicative mood)
- **Form II**: Final phrase of inconclusive sentences (subjunctive mood); all adjunctive phrases

### Phonological Description

Henderson describes the formal alternation in meticulous phonetic detail:

> "All verbs whose Form I has a long final syllable with tone 1 or 2 have tone 3 in Form II. There is no other change except that when the final consonant in Form I is a velar nasal, the final consonant in Form II is always an alveolar nasal." (p. 72)

#### Regular Verb Patterns

**Tone 1 → Tone 3 (Long Syllables)**

Phonological summary: `(C)Vc¹˙ ˉor ˙ / (C)Vc³˙ ˉor ˙ ˳`

Examples (Form I shown):
- *-kuan* 'to go to work'
- *-lu:t* 'to enter'
- *-muat* 'to decay'

**Tone 2 → Tone 3 (Long Syllables)**

Phonological summary: `(C)Vc²˙ ˉor ˙ / (C)Vc³˙ ˉor ˙`

Examples:
- *-la:m* 'to dance'
- *-bual* 'to swim'
- *-ma:n* 'to be true'

**Velar → Alveolar Final**

> "The following have a final velar nasal in Form I and a final alveolar nasal in Form II" (p. 74):

- *-xaiŋ, -xain* 'to lift up'
[≈Bible: -khaing]
- *-pa:ŋ, -pa:n* 'to defend'
- *-tua:ŋ, -tua:n* 'to perch'

**Short Syllables with Abrupt Closure (Invariable)**

> "Since short syllables with abrupt syllable closure... are precluded by their syllabic structure from the tonal variation common to other syllables, being always pronounced on a low level pitch regarded as a realization of tone 3, regular verbs whose Form I has a final syllable of this structure show no change in Form II." (p. 76)

Examples: *-nai'* 'to draw near', *-ka'* 'to receive', *-tak* 'to be right'

**Gradual → Abrupt Closure (Tone 3)**

> "Regular verbs whose Form I consists of either a long or a short tone 3 syllable with gradual closure have a short syllable with abrupt closure in Form II" (p. 77)

### Irregular Verbs

Henderson documents several patterns of irregularity:

**(a) Long/short tone 1 or 2 with gradual closure → short abrupt**

| Form I | Form II | Gloss |
|--------|---------|-------|
| *-nuam* | *-nop* | 'to want' |
| *-nu:i* | *-nui'* | 'to laugh' |
| *-si:* | *-si'* | 'to die' |
| *-ya:* | *-yai'* | 'to love' |

**(b) Open syllables acquiring final stop**

| Form I | Form II | Gloss |
|--------|---------|-------|
| *-phe:* | *-phe:t* | 'to tremble' |
| *-pha:* | *-phat* | 'to be good' |
| *-sa:* | *-sak* | 'to sing' |
| *-ne:* | *-ne:k* | 'to eat' |
| *-la:* | *-la:k* | 'to take' |

**(c) Final stop → glottal stop**

| Form I | Form II | Gloss |
|--------|---------|-------|
| *-suak* | *-sua'* | 'to be born' |

### Derived Verbs

> "A few verbs which appear at first sight to be irregular may be regarded as derived from other verbs and hence as in a class of their own. Form I of the derived verb is identical with Form II of the original verb." (p. 82)

Example: *-dam* 'to be well' (Form II: *-dam*) vs. *dam* 'to heal (tr.)' (both forms *-dam*)

## Zam Ngaih Cing 2018

**Source:** Zam Ngaih Cing. 2018. *A Descriptive Grammar of Tedim Chin*. PhD dissertation, North-Eastern Hill University. Chapter 5.

Zam Ngaih Cing uses the terminology "Stem 1" and "Stem 2" and provides a modern reanalysis that emphasizes morphosyntactic conditioning over phonological description.

### Definition

> "Most of the verbs in Tedim Chin are monosyllabic. Verbs can be divided into two main types depending on whether the stem alternates or not. The two types are: alternating verb stem and non-alternating verb stem." (p. 139)

> "Button (2011: 31) undoubtedly stated that 'Most Northern Chin verbs have a basic form FORM-I; and an inflected form FORM-II; specific syntactic functions vary between languages.' Tedim Chin, a northern Kuki-Chin language, exhibits the two forms of verbs which are labelled as Stem 1 and Stem 2 in the present study." (p. 139)

### Processes of Alternation

> "Cing (2012) discussed three main processes which triggers verb stem alternation at the segmental level in Tedim Chin. These are **addition**, **substitution** and **tonal change**." (p. 139)

> "The pattern of verb inflection is not predictable in Tedim Chin. The verbs which end with open syllables become closed with final consonants such as /p/, /t/, /k/ and /ʔ/ in coda position. The verbs with closed syllables in Stem 1 are substituted by other consonants in Stem 2." (p. 140)

### Syntactic Distribution

Zam Ngaih Cing provides detailed syntactic environments:

**Stem 1 environments (§5.5):**
- Imperative constructions (§5.5.1)
- Declarative affirmative (§5.5.2)
- Interrogative (§5.5.3)
- Conditional protasis (§5.5.5)
- With causative suffix *-sàk* (§5.8.2.2)
[≈Bible: -sak]
- Pre-verbal directionals (§5.8.1.3)

**Stem 2 environments:**
- Negative clauses (§5.5.4): "The Stem 2 verb occurs before the negative marker"
- With benefactive *-sàk* (§6.6.1.2.1): "When Stem 2 verb occurs with the suffix *-sàk*, it acts as a valence increasing operator and gives a benefactive meaning"
[≈Bible: -sak -sak]
- With comitative *-pìɁ* (§6.6.1.2.2): "A comitative applicative occurs after Stem 2 verb"
[≈Bible: -pih]
- Nominalization (§3.3.6.5.2.1): "In nominalization, the verb is in Stem 2"

### Adjective Stem Alternation

Zam Ngaih Cing also documents that adjectives show the same alternation pattern:

> "As in verbs, the adjectives can take two forms, appearing as Stem 1 and Stem 2, depending on the syntactic construction. These changes in shape involve tone, segment alternation etc. or no change at all." (p. 133)

## Singh 2018 (Sukte)

**Source:** Singh, Arambam Sadananda. 2018. *A Descriptive Grammar of Sukte (Salhte)*. PhD dissertation, Assam University.

Sukte (Salhte) is closely related to Tedim. Singh does not explicitly discuss verb stem alternation as a topic, but his examples implicitly show the phenomenon, indicating that the same grammatical patterns operate in this sister language.

## Button 2011 (Proto Northern Chin)

**Source:** Button, Christopher. 2011. *Proto Northern Chin*. PhD dissertation, SOAS.

Button reconstructs the stem alternation pattern for Proto Northern Chin (PNC):

> "Most Northern Chin verbs have a basic form FORM-I; and an inflected form FORM-II; specific syntactic functions vary between languages." (p. 31)

This establishes that verb stem alternation is a inherited feature of the entire Northern Chin subgroup, not an innovation in Tedim alone.

## Summary: Points of Agreement

1. **Universal presence**: All scholars agree that Tedim Chin verbs exhibit two distinct forms.

2. **Grammatical conditioning**: The choice of form is grammatically determined, not lexically arbitrary.

3. **Predictability**: For most verbs ("regular verbs"), Form II/Stem 2 can be predicted from Form I/Stem 1.

4. **Primary processes**:
   - Tonal change (typically high/mid → low)
   - Velar nasal → alveolar nasal
   - Open syllable → closed (addition of /ʔ/, /k/, /t/)
   - Final stop substitution (/p/ → /ʔ/, /k/ → /ʔ/, etc.)

5. **Functional correlation**: Form I/Stem 1 is associated with main clause, indicative, imperative; Form II/Stem 2 with dependent clause, subjunctive, negative, nominalized.

## Points of Difference

1. **Terminology**: Henderson's "Form I/II" vs. Zam Ngaih Cing's "Stem 1/2"

2. **Primary domain**: Henderson emphasizes phonological description; Zam Ngaih Cing emphasizes morphosyntactic conditioning.

3. **Mood association**: Henderson directly links Form to mood (indicative/subjunctive); Zam Ngaih Cing treats mood separately but notes the correlations.

## Gaps in the Literature

1. **Historical explanation**: Why does Tedim have this alternation? Button (2011) shows it's inherited from PNC, but the Proto-Kuki-Chin or Proto-Tibeto-Burman origins are not fully explained.

2. **Semantic/pragmatic factors**: Are there discourse or information-structure factors beyond the syntactic ones?

3. **Diachronic change**: Is the system stable or changing in modern spoken Tedim?

4. **Adjective alternation**: Why do adjectives show the same pattern? Is this evidence of a historical verb-adjective continuum?
