# Literature Review: Case Marking in Tedim Chin

This report compiles previous scholarship on case markers and postpositions in Tedim Chin, organized chronologically by source.

---

## 1. Henderson (1965)

### 1.1 Approach to Case

Henderson uses "case" differently from modern typological terminology. In her analysis, **case is a characteristic of the nominal figure**, specifically the formal relationship between consecutive nouns.

> "Where this formal variation (which is described on p. 70) expresses a grammatical relationship between placed elements of the nominal figure, this relationship is stated as one of case." (p. 59)

### 1.2 Direct vs. Oblique Case

Henderson posits a two-way distinction:

| Case | Form | Usage |
|------|------|-------|
| Direct | Unmarked | Most linguistic situations |
| Oblique | Marked (') | Genitival constructions |

> "When two consecutive nouns within the same nominal figure are both in the direct form, the case relationship between them is described as direct; when the first noun is in the oblique form, the case relationship is described as oblique." (p. 59)

### 1.3 The Oblique Form

The oblique form is realized by an apostrophe in the orthography, representing a tonal or phonological distinction:

> "This is recognized orthographically by the writing of an apostrophe after the noun concerned, e.g. 'Dahpa' khuang' Dahpa's drum." (p. 59)

Examples from Henderson:
- `Dahpa' khuang` "Dahpa's drum" (OBLIQUE + NOUN)
- `gal-te` "enemies" vs. `gal'-pa` "an enemy" (OBLIQUE + SUFFIX)
- `mi` "person" vs. `mi'-hin` "human being" (OBLIQUE + VERB)

### 1.4 Post-Nominal Particles

Henderson documents several particles that follow nouns, though she does not analyze them as case markers in the modern sense:

| Particle | Function (Henderson's terms) |
|----------|------------------------------|
| in | Post-nominal particle (see p. 104) |
| tawh/toh | Post-nominal particle |
| ah | Post-nominal particle |

### 1.5 Limitations

Henderson's analysis is primarily phonological/structural rather than semantic. She does not systematically describe ergative, locative, ablative, or other case functions that later researchers identify.

## 2. Zam Ngaih Cing (2017/2018)

Zam Ngaih Cing provides the most comprehensive treatment of case in Tedim Chin, identifying **seven cases** (§3.3.3.3).

### 2.1 Case Inventory

| Case | Marker | Form |
|------|--------|------|
| Ergative | -ìn | suffix |
| Instrumental | -à | suffix |
| Locative | -àɁ / -ā | suffix |
| Genitive | -á | suffix/tone |
| Ablative | -pàn | suffix |
| Comitative | -tɔ̀Ɂ | suffix |
| Dative | -ádíŋ | suffix |

### 2.2 Case Syncretism

Zam Ngaih Cing notes:

> "Case syncretism is observed in instrumental and genitive cases. However, these cases can be distinguished from each other only by their context of usage."

Both are marked by **-a**, distinguished only by context.

### 2.3 Ergative -ìn (§3.3.3.3.1)

> "Ergative case marker occurs in a transitive sentence. It marks the subject of a transitive verb."

Examples:
- (150) `à pá-ín à ínsàk-té meìtʰaì xát ŋaī aìɁ...`
  - "Her father loved their neighbor who is a widow..."
- (151) `leì xàt ɔ́m ín leì sāŋ máɁ~màɁ xàt ɔ́m ín`
  - "There was a bridge, a very high bridge was there" (INTRANSITIVE - no ERG)

### 2.4 Instrumental -à (§3.3.3.3.2)

Following Crystal (2003), Zam Ngaih Cing defines instrumental as marking "the means of accomplishment":

- (152) `...āk-ciáŋ-tɔ̀n-ā ná nā sát-lùm`
  - "...hit him dead with a long stick"

### 2.5 Locative -àɁ/-ā (§3.3.3.3.3)

The locative indicates location or destination:

- (153) `lɔú laī-xát-ā ɔ́m zaí~zaí mù`
  - "He saw them actively working in the middle of the field"
- (154) `tuā á kì-sìl-nā-áɁ à sám sɔ́p-nā-áɁ...`
  - "In the place where she took bath, where she washed her hair..."

### 2.6 Genitive -á (§3.3.3.3.4)

The genitive marks ownership and is expressed by:
1. The suffix -á
2. High tone on pronouns/names
3. Possessive pronouns (kà, nà, à)

Examples:
- (155) `híɁ kà ín` "This is my house"
- (157) `uí-ín à pū tʰeì` "The dog knows its master"
- (158) `híɁ keí ín` "This is my house" (GEN via high tone on pronoun)

### 2.7 Ablative -pàn (§3.3.3.3.5)

The ablative marks the starting point:

- (159) `lámká-pán dēlí-àɁ mí támpí pēm úɁ`
  - "Many people migrated from Lamka to Delhi"
- (160) `...à leì búl-pàn ā kúm-sùk...`
  - "...came down from the bridge..."

### 2.8 Comitative -tɔ̀Ɂ (§3.3.3.3.6)

The comitative expresses accompaniment ("with"):

> "Crystal (2003) defined comitative as the form taken by a noun phrase when it is expressing the meaning 'along with' or 'accompanied by'."

- (161) `mànnɔú-ín à naú-nú-tɔ̀Ɂ tuí tɔī`
  - "Manno carries water with her sister"
- (162) `á ván-tɔ̀Ɂ ámàɁ-tɔ̀Ɂ à nuaí làk...`
  - "With her luggage, with her, she fell down..."

### 2.9 Dative -ádíŋ (§3.3.3.3.7)

The dative marks beneficiary/purpose ("for"):

- (163) `paūpū-ín à tápā á-díŋ xèdàp tʰák leì-sàk`
  - "Paupu bought a new shoe for his son"
- (164) `táŋ xàt à ú-á díŋ ā xūk-xát tùɁ...`
  - "He sowed one seed for his brother..."

### 2.10 Unique Contributions

1. **Seven-case system**: First systematic inventory
2. **Syncretism noted**: Instrumental = Genitive formally
3. **Multiple genitive strategies**: suffix, tone, possessive pronouns
4. **Dative as GEN+PURP**: Notes compositional nature of -ádíŋ

## 3. Singh (2018): Sukte Grammar [Comparative]

Singh documents an **eight-case system** in Sukte, providing valuable comparative data.

### 3.1 Case Inventory

| Case | Sukte Marker | Tedim Equivalent |
|------|--------------|------------------|
| Nominative | -in | -ìn (Ergative) |
| Accusative | ∅ | ∅ |
| Locative | -a? | -àɁ |
| Genitive | -a | -á |
| Instrumental | -toʔ | -tɔ̀Ɂ (?) |
| Associative | -toʔ | -tɔ̀Ɂ (Comitative) |
| Ablative | -pan | -pàn |
| Dative | -kiangaʔ | -ádíŋ (?) |

### 3.2 Nominative -in (§4.5.1)

Singh calls this "nominative," but functionally it is ergative:

> "The nominative case usually indicates the subject of the sentence. In Sukte, the nominative case is expressed by the suffix -in."

- (5) `kei-in vəsə kə mu hi` "I see the bird"
- (6) `ko-in vəsə kə mu uʔ hi` "We see the bird"

### 3.3 Key Differences from Tedim

| Feature | Tedim | Sukte | Notes |
|---------|-------|-------|-------|
| ERG/NOM terminology | Ergative | Nominative | Same marker -in |
| Instrumental | -à | -toʔ | Different markers |
| Comitative | -tɔ̀Ɂ | -toʔ | Same marker? |
| Dative | -ádíŋ | -kiangaʔ | Different markers |

### 3.4 Implications

1. **Core cases cognate**: ERG -in, LOC -a?, GEN -a, ABL -pan
2. **Peripheral cases differ**: DAT, INSTR show more variation
3. **Syncretism patterns**: Both languages show case syncretism

## 4. Otsuka's Papers (2009, 2022, 2024)

### 4.1 Ergative Case (Causative-Benefactive Paper, 2009)

Otsuka notes the ergative pattern in causative constructions:

> "The subject of the causative construction, i.e. causer, is marked with the ergative case marker -in."

### 4.2 Absolutive (Zero) Case

Otsuka observes that patients and causees receive zero marking:

> "The causee and the patient are both marked with absolutive case (zero marking) in Tiddim Chin."

### 4.3 Oblique Case with kiàŋ

For three-place predicates, recipients may be marked with the postposition kiàŋ "around":

> "If the three-place verbs such as 'ask' and 'give' are causitivized by suffixing -sàk, the recipient argument is obliquely expressed with the particle kiàŋ 'around'."

- (29) `liànpí: ìn tua tʰú: huáikim kiàŋ kì:kìm dòŋ -sàk`
  - "Lianpi made Kikim inquire that matter of Huai Kim"

## 5. Synthesis and Comparison

### 5.1 Case Marker Correspondence

| Function | Henderson | Zam Ngaih Cing | Sukte (Singh) | Otsuka |
|----------|-----------|----------------|---------------|--------|
| Ergative/Agent | 'in' (particle) | -ìn | -in | -in |
| Absolutive | (direct) | ∅ | ∅ | ∅ |
| Genitive | oblique (') | -á | -a | — |
| Locative | 'ah' | -àɁ/-ā | -a? | — |
| Ablative | — | -pàn | -pan | — |
| Comitative | 'tawh' | -tɔ̀Ɂ | -toʔ | — |
| Instrumental | — | -à | -toʔ | — |
| Dative | — | -ádíŋ | -kiangaʔ | kiàŋ |

### 5.2 Alignment Type

All sources confirm **ergative-absolutive alignment**:
- Transitive subjects: marked with -in
- Intransitive subjects & transitive objects: unmarked (∅)

### 5.3 Case Syncretism

| Syncretic Cases | Marker | Distinguished by |
|-----------------|--------|------------------|
| Instrumental/Genitive | -a | Context |
| Comitative/Instrumental (Sukte) | -toʔ | Context |

### 5.4 Key Distinctions by Author

| Feature | Henderson | Zam Ngaih Cing | Singh (Sukte) |
|---------|-----------|----------------|---------------|
| Number of cases | 2 (direct/oblique) | 7 | 8 |
| Ergative analysis | No | Yes | (calls it nominative) |
| Syncretism noted | No | Yes | No |
| Compositional dative | No | Yes (-á + -díŋ) | No |
| Oblique locative (kiàŋ) | No | No | No (Otsuka does) |

### 5.5 Evolution of Analysis

1. **Henderson (1965)**: Structuralist, phonological focus. Two-case system (direct/oblique) based on noun form rather than semantic role.

2. **Zam Ngaih Cing (2018)**: Modern typological framework. Seven-case system with clear semantic definitions. Notes syncretism.

3. **Singh (2018)**: Comparative data from Sukte. Eight cases, though some may be redundant (nominative = accusative markedness pattern).

4. **Otsuka (2009)**: Confirms ergative-absolutive pattern. Documents oblique marking strategies (kiàŋ).

## 6. Open Questions

1. **Ergative optionality**: Is -ìn always required on transitive subjects, or is it optional in some contexts? (Split-ergativity?)

2. **Genitive strategies**: What determines choice between suffix -á, high tone, and possessive pronoun?

3. **Instrumental vs. Comitative**: Both can mean "with" — what is the semantic distinction?

4. **Locative variants**: Is -àɁ vs. -ā a free variant, or does it encode a semantic distinction (goal vs. location)?

5. **Dative decomposition**: If -ádíŋ = GEN + PURP, is it a true case marker or a complex postposition?
