# Literature Review: Sentence-Final Particles in Tedim Chin

This report compiles previous scholarship on sentence-final particles in Tedim Chin.

---

## 1. Henderson (1965)

### 1.1 Sentence Particles (pp. 30-31)

> "In the narrative style of these texts only two sentence particles are recognized, both occurring finally, namely **'hi'** and **'leh'**."

**Conclusive** sentences end with **hi**:
- Majority of sentences in texts
- Characterized by **indicative mood** (Form I verbs)

**Inconclusive** sentences end with **leh**:
- Form compound sentences with following conclusive sentence
- Characterized by **subjunctive mood** (Form II verbs)

> "A sentence containing the sentence particle 'hi' may be termed conclusive; one containing the sentence particle 'leh' may be termed inconclusive."

### 1.2 Sentence Intonation (pp. 30-31)

> "Characteristic of the sentence are the following intonational features: (a) the gradual downward drift, by which each succeeding phrase tends to be pitched in a key slightly lower than the preceding one; and (b) the pitch-contour of the final sentence particle or particle group."

**Low sentence intonation**:
- Low falling or low level pitch on **hi**
- Low level pitch on **leh** (some cases)

**High sentence intonation**:
- High level or short high rising pitch on **leh**
- Restricted to inconclusive sentences

### 1.3 Mood and Sentence Type

> "In the narrative style, the final predicative phrase of all conclusive sentences contains a verb or verbs in Form I... Such sentences are said to be characterized by the **indicative mood**."

> "The final verbal phrase of all inconclusive sentences contains a verb or verbs in Form II... Such sentences are said to be characterized by the **subjunctive mood**."

## 2. Zam Ngaih Cing (2017/2018)

### 2.1 Declarative Markers (§7.4)

> "In Tedim Chin, the declarative clause is marked in informal speech with **'e'** or **'ei'** which both occurs at the final position. 'e' is the more polite form of marking declarative and 'ei' gives a strong assertion as compared to 'e'."

```
dām [≈dam]    kīm [≈kim]       úŋ [≈ung]       ē
heal.1 complete  1PL.RLS  DECL
'We are all fine' (polite)

dām [≈dam]    kīm [≈kim]       úŋ [≈ung]       eí
heal.1 complete  1PL.RLS  DECL
'We are all fine' (strong assertion)
```

### 2.2 Person Agreement Markers (§5.8.2.5)

> "Person agreement can occur in sentence final position. It varies according to person and number."

**Table 35: Person agreement**

| | Singular | Plural |
|---|----------|--------|
| First person | iŋ [≈ing] (RLS) / niŋ [≈ning] (IRR) | uŋ [≈ung] (excl.) / haŋ [≈hang] (incl.) |
| Second person | teɁ [≈teh] | uɁteɁ [≈uhteh] |

Example from narrative:
```
vàn [≈van]-ā     āksínélkaī [≈aksinelkai]  deìɁ-laī    íŋ [≈ing]       cì [≈ci]    mɔ́k [≈mawk]
sky-LOC   comet       want-PROG   1SG.RLS  say.1 INTJ
'She said she still wanted comet from the sky'
```

### 2.3 Imperative Markers (§5.7.1)

> "An imperative mood occurs with Stem 1 verb. It is marked by **ɔù [≈o]**, **dìɁ [≈dih]** and **vē [≈ve]**."

```
paī   ɔú [≈o]
go.1  IMP
'Go'

tú [≈tu]    vē [≈ve]
sit.1 IMP
'Sit'
```

### 2.4 Interrogative Particles (§7.4.1.3)

See dedicated report on interrogatives. Key particles:
- **hiam / hia** — question particle
- **diam / dia** — irrealis question (< díŋ [≈ding] + hiam)
- **m / mɔ̀ [≈maw]** — simple question

## 3. Singh (2018): Sukte Grammar [Comparative]

### 3.1 Declarative Marker hi (§8.1.1)

> "Declarative clause in Sukte is expressed by the declarative suffix **hi**."

```
əməʔ [≈amah]-in   ə      iʔmu [≈ihmu]    ta    hὶ
he-NOM    3SG    sleep   PST   DECL
'He slept.'

john-in   hai     ə     ne    hὶ
John-NOM  mango   3SG   eat   DECL
'John eats mango.'
```

### 3.2 Imperative Marker in (§8.1.2)

```
hiʔ [≈hih]   laibu   sim   in
this  book    read  IMP
'Read this book.'

tu    in
sit   IMP
'Sit down.'
```

### 3.3 Question Particles (§8.1.3)

- **hiam** — general question particle
- **diam** — irrealis question (< diŋ [≈ding] + hiam)

```
nəŋ [≈nang]   saŋsia [≈sangsia]   nə [≈na]    hiam?
you   teacher  2SG   QM
'Are you teacher?'
```

### 3.4 Wish Marker təhen (§8.1.2.3)

```
pasian-in   nəŋ [≈nang]   hoŋ [≈hong]   hon   təhen [≈tahen]
God-NOM     you   DIR   save  WISH
'May God save you.'
```

## 4. Synthesis

### 4.1 Sentence-Final Particle Inventory

| Function | Tedim | Sukte | Henderson Term |
|----------|-------|-------|----------------|
| Declarative | hi | hi | Conclusive |
| Sequential | leh | — | Inconclusive |
| Polite DECL | e | — | — |
| Strong DECL | ei | — | — |
| Imperative | ɔù [≈o], dìɁ [≈dih], vē [≈ve] | in | — |
| Question | hiam, m | hiam | — |
| Irrealis Q | diam | diam | — |
| Wish | — | təhen [≈tahen] | — |

### 4.2 Person Agreement as Sentence-Final

Tedim Chin has **person agreement markers** that appear sentence-finally:

| | Realis | Irrealis |
|---|--------|----------|
| 1SG | iŋ [≈ing] | niŋ [≈ning] |
| 1PL.EXCL | uŋ [≈ung] | — |
| 1PL.INCL | haŋ [≈hang] | — |
| 2SG | teɁ [≈teh] | — |
| 2PL | uɁteɁ [≈uhteh] | — |

### 4.3 Mood-Particle Correlations (Henderson)

| Sentence Type | Particle | Verb Form | Mood |
|---------------|----------|-----------|------|
| Conclusive | hi | Form I | Indicative |
| Inconclusive | leh | Form II | Subjunctive |

### 4.4 Distinctive Findings

**Henderson's unique contributions**:
1. **Conclusive vs. inconclusive** sentence dichotomy
2. **Mood marking** (indicative vs. subjunctive) tied to sentence particles
3. **Sentence intonation** (high vs. low) correlated with particle type

**Zam Ngaih Cing's unique contributions**:
1. **Register distinction**: e (polite) vs. ei (assertive)
2. **Person agreement markers** as sentence-final elements
3. **Realis/irrealis** distinction in person markers

**Singh's contribution**:
- Confirms cognate declarative marker hi across Kuki-Chin

## 5. Open Questions

1. **e/ei distribution**: What contexts trigger each declarative marker?
2. **leh grammaticalization**: Is leh related to leɁ [≈leh] 'if' (conditional)?
3. **Person markers**: Are these clitics or independent particles?
4. **Evidentiality**: Are there evidential distinctions in sentence-final position?
5. **Mirativity**: Are there markers for unexpected information?
