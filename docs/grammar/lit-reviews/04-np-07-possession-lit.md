# Literature Review: Possession in Tedim Chin

This report compiles previous scholarship on possession marking in Tedim Chin.

---

## 1. Henderson (1965)

Henderson discusses pronominal prefixes and their concordance relationships but does not provide a dedicated section on possession. She notes:

> "The pronominal prefix in 'Dahpa a hi hi' is ambiguous, as formally it could concord with 'Dahpa'. The only formal expression of the absence of concord in the sentence cited is the use of the oblique form of the noun." (p. 66)

This suggests awareness of possessive prefix patterns without systematic treatment.

## 2. Zam Ngaih Cing (2017/2018)

### 2.1 Possessive Pronouns (§3.2.2)

> "A possessive pronoun deals with possession. [...] There are three main types of marking possession depending on the type of person."

**Table 22: Possessive Pronouns**

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| First | kà [≈ka] 'my' | ì 'our (excl.)', eí 'our (incl.)' |
| Second | nà [≈na] 'your' | — |
| Third | à 'his/her' | — |

**Key observation**: Tedim Chin has an **inclusive-exclusive distinction** in first person plural possession.

### 2.2 Inherently vs. Optionally Possessed Nouns (§3.2.2)

> "Tedim Chin, like many other languages, has inherently and optionally possessed nouns. Optionally possessed nouns include concrete nouns. Payne (1997) describes inherently possessed nouns to normally include body parts and kinship terms."

Example:
```
kà [≈ka]          pí [≈pi]                  (inherently possessed)
1POSS       grandmother
'my grandmother'
```

### 2.3 Tonal Possession Marking (§2.3.8)

**Zam Ngaih Cing's most distinctive contribution**: Tone as possessive marker.

> "In Tedim Chin, there is change of tone in personal names when it is used to mark possession. Thus, tones in Tedim Chin have a grammatical function, that is, it acts as a possessive marker."

**Personal names**:
```
liànpū [≈npu]      → liànpú [≈npu] (with rising tone)
'Lianpu'      'Lianpu's'

cı̄ŋnɔ́ú [≈cingno]      → cı̄ŋnɔ́ú [≈cingno] (with rising tone)
'Cingnu'      'Cingnu's'
```

**Personal pronouns**:
```
keí         → keí (with tonal modification)
'I'           functions as 'my' (= kà [≈ka])
```

> "So it can be said that tone is also one of the possessive markers in Tedim Chin."

### 2.4 Genitive Case (§3.3.3.3.4)

> "Genitive case is marked by -á [≈-a]. It marks for ownership. The possessive pronouns such as kà [≈ka], nà [≈na] and à also represents ownership for first person, second person and third person respectively."

**Three strategies for marking possession**:
1. Possessive pronouns: kà [≈ka], nà [≈na], à
2. Genitive suffix: -á [≈-a]
3. Tonal modification on personal names/pronouns

Example with genitive:
```
keí         á
1SG.GEN     GEN
'mine/belonging to me'
```

## 3. Singh (2018): Sukte Grammar [Comparative]

### 3.1 Pronominal Possession (§4.5.4)

> "Possession is indicated by the pronominal prefixes in Sukte."

| Prefix | Gloss |
|--------|-------|
| kə [≈ka]- | 1SG |
| nə [≈na]- | 2SG |
| ə- | 3SG |

### 3.2 Kinship Terms (§4.2)

> "The kin roots in Sukte are bound in forms, without pronominal prefixes the entire kinship terms never be free in form."

| Form | Gloss |
|------|-------|
| kə [≈ka]-nu | 'my mother' |
| kə [≈ka]-pa | 'my father' |
| nə [≈na]-nu | 'your mother' |
| ə-pa | 'his/her father' |

### 3.3 Body Parts

> "In Sukte, the body parts of humans and animals are usually possessed [with] pronominal clitics. [...] most of roots relating to body parts in Sukte are bound."

| Form | Gloss |
|------|-------|
| ə-keŋ [≈-keng] | 'his/her foot' |
| nə [≈na]-khut | 'your hand' |
| kə [≈ka]-keŋ [≈-keng] | 'my foot' |
| ə-ŋoŋ [≈-ngong] | 'his/her neck' |

### 3.4 Genitive Case Marker -i (§4.5.4)

> "The genitive case in Sukte is expressed by the marker i to indicate the possession of a thing or a person."

```
hi?      merry-i        in    hi
this     Merry-GEN      house COP
'This is Merry's house.'
```

## 4. Synthesis

### 4.1 Possessive Prefixes

| | Tedim | Sukte |
|---|-------|-------|
| 1SG | kà [≈ka] | kə [≈ka] |
| 2SG | nà [≈na] | nə [≈na] |
| 3SG | à | ə |
| 1PL.EXCL | ì | — |
| 1PL.INCL | eí | — |

### 4.2 Possession Strategies

| Strategy | Tedim | Sukte |
|----------|-------|-------|
| Pronominal prefixes | ✓ | ✓ |
| Genitive suffix | -á [≈-a] | -i |
| Tonal marking | ✓ (ZNC) | Not documented |

### 4.3 Inalienable Possession

Both languages treat **kinship terms** and **body parts** as inherently possessed (bound roots requiring possessive prefixes).

### 4.4 Distinctive Findings

**Zam Ngaih Cing's unique contribution**:
- Documents **tonal possession marking** on personal names
- Rising tone on final syllable indicates possession
- This is not documented in other sources

**Singh's contribution**:
- Documents genitive marker -i in Sukte (cognate with Tedim -á [≈-a]?)
- Shows systematic bound roots for kin terms and body parts

## 5. Open Questions

1. **Tonal possession**: Is this productive or limited to certain contexts?
2. **Genitive cognates**: Are Tedim -á [≈-a] and Sukte -i historically related?
3. **Alienability**: Is there a systematic alienable/inalienable distinction beyond kin terms and body parts?
4. **Plural possession**: How is plural possessor marked when possessee is inherently possessed?
