## -sak (CAUS/BENF)

### Overview

The suffix -sak is the most discussed morpheme in Tedim Chin literature due to its dual function as both causative and benefactive marker, distinguished by verb stem form.

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Form | -sak (tone varies by context) |
| Category | Derivational suffix |
| Functions | Causative (Form I), Benefactive (Form II) |
| Productivity | Highly productive |
| Sukte cognate | sək (causative only) |

### Henderson (1965: pp. 82-85)

Henderson provides the foundational analysis of -sak, treating it as a **verb** (later disputed by Otsuka).

**Key insight:** The distinction between causative and benefactive readings depends on verb stem form:

> "Compare here 'pail! sak in' go on his behalf with 'pail sak in' let him go. In an indicative sentence, therefore, 'sak' is to be translated as to let or cause to be done and in a subjunctive sentence by to do on someone else's behalf." (p. 84)

**Phonological note:**
> "Forms I and II in a verb of the phonological structure of 'sak' are identical (see p. 76), but Form I is postulated for the indicative sentence and Form II for the subjunctive sentence." (p. 84)

**Henderson's Examples:**

*Indicative (Form I + -sak = CAUSATIVE):*
- `pai sak in` "let him go"
[≈Bible: pai sak in]

*Subjunctive (Form II + -sak = BENEFACTIVE):*
- `paì sàk in` "go on his behalf"
[≈Bible: pai sak in]
- `kong màt sàk hi` "I've caught it for you"
[≈Bible: kong mat sak hi]
- `na lo kong khàwh sàk hi` "I'll cultivate your fields for you"
[≈Bible: na lo kong khawh sak hi]
- `a dàm sàk hi` "he healed him"
[≈Bible: a dam sak hi]

**Historical note (p. 83):**
Henderson observes that -sak is replacing older morphological causatives:
> "people nowadays tended to use the verb 'sak' to cause to be done to replace the causative derivative"

She gives older causative pairs being replaced:
- `kia` "to fall" ~ `xia` "to drop"
- `ka:k` "to dilate (intr.)" ~ `xark` "to open wide (tr.)"
- `purk` "to fall" ~ `phu:k` "to fell"

### Otsuka (2009: full article)

Otsuka's specialized study is the most comprehensive treatment of -sak. He argues contra Henderson that -sak is a **bound suffix**, not a verb.

**Morphological Status (pp. 9-12):**

Otsuka's classification: -sak is a BOUND MORPHEME/SUFFIX (not a verb)

Reasons given:
1. No lexical meaning on its own
2. Always occurs immediately after verb
3. Cannot stand alone
4. Doesn't have two alternative verb stems (unlike verbs)
5. Causative and benefactive -sak never co-occur (suggesting single morpheme)

> "It is proposed that -sàk is a bound morpheme not a verb, because no lexical meaning is observed on -sàk alone and -sàk always occurs immediately after the verb." (p. 9)

**Why one morpheme, not two homophonous morphemes:**
1. Benefactive and causative -sak never co-occur
2. Similar single morpheme in other languages (e.g., Indonesian -kan)
3. Logical to treat as single morpheme with context-dependent meaning

**Form I + -sak (CAUSATIVE) Examples:**

(25) `nú guàʔ tèɕí ɔ̀ʔ tùŋ-sàk`
[≈Bible: nu guah tecii awh tung-sak]
aunt Guah pot COP arrive.I-SAK
"Aunty Guah made the pot arrive"

(27) `kèn pú túi tùŋ-sàk`
[≈Bible: ken pu tui tung-sak]
1SG.ERG grandfather water drink.I-SAK
"I made grandfather drink water"

**Form II + -sak (BENEFACTIVE) Examples:**

(30) `kèn liàn niaŋtú:i bò:l-sàk`
[≈Bible: ken lian niangtui bawl-sak]
1SG.ERG Lian tea make.II-SAK
"I made some tea for Lian"

(31) `kèn liàn niaŋtú:i kʰù-sàk`
[≈Bible: ken lian niangtui khu-sak]
1SG.ERG Lian tea carry.II-SAK
"I carried tea for Lian"

**Inverse Marking (pp. 10-11):**

The prefix hong- (Otsuka writes óŋ-) is obligatory when causee/beneficiary is 1st/2nd person:

(23) `nú hà:u ìn kéi óŋ-án huán-sàk`
[≈Bible: nu hau in kei hong-an huan-sak]
aunt Hau ERG 1SG INV-meal cook.I-SAK
"Aunty Hau made me cook a meal" (CAUSATIVE)

(24) `nú hà:u ìn kéi óŋ-án huàn-sàk`
[≈Bible: nu hau in kei hong-an huan-sak]
aunt Hau ERG 1SG INV-meal cook.II-SAK
"Aunty Hau cooked a meal for me" (BENEFACTIVE)

**Fossilized benefactive verbs (pp. 9-10):**

Some Form II verbs show optional -sak:
- `gòʔ (-sàk)` slaughter.II "to slaughter something for someone"
- `dèt (-sàk)` lighten.II "to light something for someone"
- `zòn (-sàk)` search.II "to search something for someone"

Henderson (1965) observed this is no longer productive; modern speakers use -sak to replace these.

**Double causative (p. 19):**

Rare reduplication for double causation:
`pai -sák -sàk òu` "Make B ask C to go!"
[≈Bible: pai -sak -sak ou]
Three participants: A (listener/outer causer), B (middle causee/causer), C (inner causee)

**Cross-linguistic comparison (pp. 19-20):**

| Language | Causative | Benefactive |
|----------|-----------|-------------|
| Tedim Chin | -sak (Form I) | -sak (Form II) |
| Haka Lai | -ter/-tsiʔ | -piak/-hnoʔ |
| Daai Chin | -shak | -pe:t/-pe |
| Thado Kuki | -sak | -peq |

> "Tiddim is exceptional in using same suffix; others have distinct morphemes for causative vs benefactive" (p. 20)

### ZNC (2017/2018: Part 2, §5.8.2.2, pp. 62-82)

Zam Ngaih Cing provides the most comprehensive modern treatment with extensive examples.

**Classification:**
> "Another type of causatives that occur in Tedim Chin is morphological causative. It is expressed by the suffix -sàk. This causative is more common and productive in Tedim Chin as compared to lexical causatives." (p. 65)

**Key statement:**
> "When Stem1 verb is suffixed with -sàk, the sentence becomes causative. Stem 2 verb gives the meaning 'for the benefit of somebody' when it is suffixed by -sàk. Thus, it is observed that -sàk can give both causative and benefactive meaning." (p. 65)

**CAUSATIVE Examples (Form I + -sak):**

(359) `kà pū tuí dn-sák ní`
[≈Bible: ka pu tui dan-sak ni]
1POSS grandfather water drink.I-CAUS HORT
"Let's make/let my grandfather drink water"

(360) `híʔ pá-n ɔ́ŋ hèʔ-sàk`
[≈Bible: hih pa-n hong heh-sak]
this MAS-ERG INV angry.I-CAUS
"This man makes me angry"

(430) `zātɔúpá-n dāmlɔú-té tùtná-nuám-àɁ tú-sàk`
[≈Bible: zatawpa-n damlaw-te tutna-nuam-ah tu-sak]
doctor-ERG patient-PL seat-nice-LOC sit.I-CAUS
"The doctor made/let the patients sit on a comfortable seat"

(431) `lùn nuàm-ìn á laíbú à nú puá-sàk`
[≈Bible: Lun Nuam-in a laibu a nu pua-sak]
Lun.Nuam-ERG 3POSS book 3POSS mother carry.I-CAUS
"Lun Nuam made/let her mother carry her book"

(432) `hɔìɁ kīm-ín à naú-pá kàp-sàk`
[≈Bible: Hoih Kim-in a nau-pa kap-sak]
Hoih.Kim-ERG 3POSS younger-MAS cry.I-CAUS
"Hoih Kim made/let her younger brother cry"

**BENEFACTIVE Examples (Form II + -sak):**

(361) `síŋxuàʔ kɔ́ŋ puák-sàk`
[≈Bible: singkhuah kong puak-sak]
firewood 1POSS.toward carry.II-BENF
"I carried firewood for you"

**Tonal behavior (p. 66):**

> "The causative suffix -sàk primarily has a Low tone. But it is realized as a High tone in some other context... This confirms the tonal pattern shown in Cing and Temsunungsang (2012) that Tedim Chin does not permit the combination of Mid + Low tone. So in places where the Low tone is expected to occur, it is usually replaced by a High tone."

**Causative prefix su- (pp. 66-67):**

ZNC also documents a causative prefix su-:

(433) `níták naī kuá-īn meí mìt`
[≈Bible: nitak nai kua-in mei mit]
night watch nine-ADV light off.I
"The light goes off at nine o'clock at night" (no affix, automatic)

(434) `níták naī kuá sím-ín íncíŋpá-n meí sù-mìt dèn`
[≈Bible: nitak nai kua sim-in incingpa-n mei su-mit den]
night watch nine every-ADV watchman-ERG light CAUS-off.I DUR
"At every nine o'clock at night, the watchman regularly put off the light"

The prefix su- marks volitional causation.

### Singh (2018: Sukte, pp. 220-221, §8.5)

Singh's Sukte grammar provides comparative data on the cognate morpheme.

**Key difference:** In Sukte, sək functions ONLY as causative (no benefactive sense).

> "Typologically, Sukte has morphological causative. So, causativisation in Sukte is formed by adding a causative suffix sək to the verb stem." (p. 220)

**Examples:**

(420) `əməʔ kə kəp sək hi`
he 1SG cry CAUS DECL
"I make him cry"

(421) `əməʔ kə nui sək hi`
he 1SG laugh CAUS DECL
"I make him laugh"

(422) `əməʔ lai kə sim sək hi`
he book 1SG read CAUS DECL
"I make him to read book"

(425) `kei-in-shyam zi əʔ kə pai sək hi`
I-NOM-Shyam market LOC 1SG go CAUS DECL
"I make Shyam to go to market"

**Summary (p. 263):**
> "Causativisation in Sukte is formed by adding a causative suffix sək to the verb stem. In other words, almost all the verbs in Sukte can be causitivised by postposing the same morpheme sək to the verb stem."

## -pih (APPL.COM)

### Overview

The comitative applicative suffix -pih increases verb valence by adding a comitative argument (companion or accompanying object). Unlike -sak, it attaches ONLY to Form II stems.

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Form | -pih (ZNC: -pìɁ; Otsuka: -piʔ³) |
| Category | Derivational suffix |
| Function | Comitative applicative |
| Productivity | Productive |
| Stem requirement | Form II only (strict) |
| Homophone | Nominal -pih "group member" |

### Henderson (1965)

Henderson does not discuss -pih as a verbal suffix. However, she documents a nominal suffix -pih meaning "member/group":

> -piʔ "member/group member" (p. 157)

Examples of nominal -pih:
- `a inkuan-pih te` "his family members" (3POSS family-member PL)
- `ka kho-pih` "my village-mate" (1POSS village-member)
- `na sang-pih` "your school-mate" (2POSS school-member)

### Otsuka (2024: "Applicative Constructions in Tiddim Chin")

Otsuka's dedicated paper (人文学林, Vol. 1, pp. 43-63) provides the most comprehensive analysis of -pih as one of three productive applicative markers.

**Classification:**
> "When the comitative suffix -piʔ³ is attached to Form II intransitive or transitive verbs, the verb valence increases. The newly introduced core argument (i.e., direct object) in the applicative construction represents either a comitative participant... or a comitative object." (p. 56)

**Stem Requirement:**
- -pih attaches ONLY to Form II (Stem II) verbs
- Cannot attach to Form I stems
- This distinguishes it from -sak which uses both forms

**EXAMPLE (40) - Comitative participant with intransitive verb:**

a. Non-applicative baseline:
`galkap-mangpa a pai hi`
[≈Bible: galkap-mangpa a pai hi]
soldier-officer 3 go.I COP
"The soldier-officer went."

b. Applicative with -pih:
`galkap-mangpa in sumbukpa tapa a pai-pih hi`
[≈Bible: galkap-mangpa in sumbukpa tapa a pai-pih hi]
soldier-officer ERG shop.owner.GEN son 3 go.II-COM.APPL COP
"The soldier-officer took the shop owner's son with him."

**EXAMPLE (41) - Comitative participant with transitive verb:**

a. Non-applicative baseline:
`Lian in an a ne hi`
[≈Bible: Lian in an a ne hi]
PN ERG food 3 eat.I COP
"Lian ate food."

b. Applicative with -pih:
`Lian in nu-Hau an a nek-pih hi`
[≈Bible: Lian in nu-Hau an a nek-pih hi]
PN ERG aunt-PN food 3 eat.II-COM.APPL COP
"Lian ate food together with Aunt Hau / Lian ate food (and gave some) to Aunt Hau."

**EXAMPLE (42) - Comitative object (accompanying thing):**

a. Non-applicative baseline:
`amah a in ah a ciah hi`
[≈Bible: amah a in ah a ciah hi]
3SG 3 house LOC 3 return.home.I COP
"He returned home."

b. Applicative with -pih:
`aman tua bel-sia a in ah a ciah-pih hi`
[≈Bible: aman tua bel-sia a in ah a ciah-pih hi]
3SG.ERG DEM pot-damaged 3 house LOC 3 return.home.II-COM.APPL COP
"He brought the broken pot home (with him)."

**Co-occurrence restrictions (p. 56):**
> "Neither the benefactive suffix -sak³ nor the causative suffix -sak³ co-occur with the comitative suffix -piʔ³ or the relinquitive suffix -san³."

However, -pih CAN co-occur with -san (relinquitive):
- `pai-pih-san` go.II-COM.APPL-RELINQ.APPL

**Homonymy note (Footnote 4, p. 56):**
The verbal comitative -pih is homonymous with nominal -pih "member" (as documented in Henderson 1965).

### ZNC (2017/2018: Part 2, §5.8.2.7 & §6.6.1.2.2)

**§5.8.2.7 "Comitative" (p. 164):**

> "Verb can be accompanied by a comitative suffix marked by -pìɁ. The verb is always in Stem 2 when it is used with comitative marker."

**Grammaticality contrasts:**

(375) ✓ `tut-pih` sit.II-COM "sit together"
(376) ✗ `*tu-pih` sit.I-COM [ungrammatical]

(377) ✓ `nuih-pih` laugh.II-COM "laugh together"
(378) ✗ `*nui-pih` laugh.I-COM [ungrammatical]

**§6.6.1.2.2 "Applicatives" (pp. 182-183):**

> "A comitative applicative occurs after Stem 2 verb. It is marked by -pìɁ... It is ungrammatical to use the comitative marker with Stem 1."

**EXAMPLE (443) - Comitative applicative (grammatical):**

`pa Hang-in a in-ah zin-te an nek-pih`
[≈Bible: pa Hang-in a in-ah zin-te an nek-pih]
MAS Hang-ERG 3POSS house-LOC guest-PL food eat.II-COM
"Uncle Hang ate food (together) with the guests at his house"

**EXAMPLE (444) - Stem I (ungrammatical):**

`*pa Hang-in a in-ah zin-te an ne-pih`
[marked ungrammatical - Stem I cannot take comitative]

**EXAMPLE (445) - Transitive verb with comitative:**

`Siannu-in a u-nu tui toi-pih`
[≈Bible: Siannu-in a u-nu tui toi-pih]
Siannu-ERG 3POSS elder-FEM water carry.II-COM
"Siannu carried water (together) with her sister"

**EXAMPLES (446-447) - Comitative with directional suffix:**

(446) Without comitative:
`Lian Khai lampi-ah pai-suk`
[≈Bible: Lian Khai lampi-ah pai-suk]
Lian Khai road-LOC go.I-down
"Lian Khai is walking down the road"

(447) With comitative + directional:
`Lian Khai-in a nau-nu lampi-ah pai-pih-suk`
[≈Bible: Lian Khai-in a nau-nu lampi-ah pai-pih-suk]
Lian.Khai-ERG 3POSS younger-FEM road-LOC go.II-COM-down
"Lian Khai walked down along with his younger sister on the road"
